Aerobic Exercise

{% endif %}

Often referred to as cardio or cardiovascular exercise, aerobic exercise is a type of physical activity that increases your heart rate and breathing while engaging large muscle groups. It offers numerous health benefits, including improved cardiovascular health, increased lung capacity, weight management, and enhanced overall fitness. Common types of activity are:

  • Running
  • Cycling
  • Swimming
  • Rowing
  • Skipping
  1. Warm Up: Before you start your main aerobic exercise, warm up your body with 5-10 minutes of light, low-intensity movements. This prepares your muscles and reduces the risk of injury.
  2. Duration: Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise or 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity aerobic exercise per week. You can break this into smaller sessions throughout the week.
  3. Intensity: The intensity of your aerobic exercise should make your heart rate increase and make you breathe harder. You should be able to talk, but it should be somewhat challenging.
  4. Progression: Gradually increase the duration and intensity of your workouts as your fitness level improves. This will help you continue to challenge your body.
  5. Cool Down: After your aerobic exercise, cool down with 5-10 minutes of low-intensity movements and stretching. This helps reduce muscle soreness and promotes flexibility.
  6. Frequency: Aim for at least 3-5 sessions of aerobic exercise per week to experience significant health benefits.
  7. Safety: Stay hydrated during exercise, wear appropriate clothing and footwear, and consider your surroundings for safety when exercising outdoors.
  8. Variety: Incorporate a variety of aerobic activities to prevent boredom and work different muscle groups.
  9. Listen to Your Body: Pay attention to how your body feels during exercise. If you experience pain, dizziness, or discomfort, stop and seek medical advice if necessary.

** Consistency is key to reaping the full benefits of aerobic exercise. It's also essential to consult with a healthcare provider, especially if you have any pre-existing medical conditions or are new to exercise, to ensure your exercise routine is safe and suitable for your individual needs **
